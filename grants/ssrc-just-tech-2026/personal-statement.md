# Personal Statement — SSRC Just Tech Fellowship 2027
### Jeffrey Alan Scudder · final (cap: ≤1,000 words written, or ≤5-min video)

I make software that tries to give the personal computer back to the people
using it. I trained as a painter — a BFA from Ringling, an MFA from the Yale
School of Art — and the standard a mark leaves on a surface, immediate and
yours, is still the one everything else in my practice answers to. When I moved
into software it was to make a tool feel that immediate, and somewhere in that
work I realized the tool itself was the art. For the last several years I have
built and operated **Aesthetic Computer**: a creative-computing runtime, an
operating system, and a small online society that people make and keep their
work on rather than rent.

It is not a product with users. It is closer to a place with residents. As of
this writing it carries **2,907 registered handles**, **402 community-authored
pieces**, and a continuous public record of **20,109 commits** across roughly
twenty contributors. People there have a name, a "mood" that becomes the site's
banner for the day, a chat that follows them into any program, a community news
board, and the standing to publish their own programs into shared web address
space. I built the whole thing in the open and gave it away as a commons,
because the question underneath it is not technical, it is civic: *who owns the
personal computer, and what would it take to own one in common?*

That question stopped being abstract this past year. Windows 10's end-of-life
stranded an estimated **240 million** working x86_64 laptops, and the world now
generates roughly **62 million tonnes** of e-waste annually. Meanwhile the
demand for AI compute is pulling everything toward the data center, turning the
machine on your desk into a thin client — a rented window onto someone else's
computer, with your identity kept in their cloud account. Both forces point the
same way: the personal computer, the most basic site of creative freedom,
becomes something you no longer really own or can read all the way down.

So I built the counter-move. **AC Native** is a bare-metal creative operating
system that boots a salvaged laptop from a USB stick — as the first process on
the machine, with no systemd, no shell, no consumer userland — into a full
creative environment in about seven seconds, with sound, graphics, input,
network, and your identity stored in the device rather than a login. The
per-seat cost lands near fifty dollars. A laptop the upgrade cycle threw away
becomes a serious instrument again: a 3,870-line synthesizer on it models all
128 General MIDI voices through physical modeling. And the language at the
center of the system, **KidLisp**, is deliberately tiny — 118 built-ins — so a
few lines earn a short code anyone can run, share by QR, or remix. My house
guide for the code states the value plainly: *a piece of this system should fit
in one mind.* That is an accessibility claim and a governance claim at once; a
commons can only be self-governed if its members can actually read it.

I'm applying to Just Tech because this is precisely the lane the program funds —
a practitioner building a working alternative at the intersection of technology
and society and tending the public that lives inside it — and because the fit
here is unusually direct. I am currently **Author in Residence at UCLA's Social
Software studio, hosted by Casey Reas and Lauren Lee McCarthy.** McCarthy, the
creator of p5.js, was herself a Just Tech fellow; the precedent that artist-built
software with a society-facing argument belongs in this cohort is, for me, a
person I work with every week. The KidLisp teaching cards I print and circulate
were first made for that studio.

What the fellowship year makes possible is the part I cannot do alone: turning
AC Native from a working prototype into a **public device library** — a
circulating fleet of reflashed laptops, with Flash Days and family workshops —
so that "you can own your computer again" stops being an argument and becomes
something a person in a room can hold in their hands. I already host biweekly
NELA Computer Club demos in Chinatown, Los Angeles; this is the path from a demo
to a lending program.

I'll be honest about the work's shape, because the program asks for it. AC today
has a strong central steward — me — and a real but small core of contributors.
Part of the year's explicit work is widening that: lowering the barrier to
authorship and co-stewardship, and writing the governance down so the commons
can outlast any one maintainer. A cohort of people thinking rigorously about
technology, equity, and the public is exactly the room I want to do that in, and
exactly the kind of sparring I'd bring to others' work — anyone can publish a
piece into Aesthetic Computer, so it's a hospitable substrate for collaboration,
not just a thing I show.

The throughline of my practice, from a painted mark to a kernel, has always been
*ownership and immediacy together* — the whole stack in hand. What's changed is
the stakes. Once other people make and keep their work on what you've built, it
becomes a commons; and as computing gets pulled into the data center, holding
onto a computer you actually own stops being a preference and becomes something
worth defending. I'd rather hand a community a living, repeatable commons than a
sealed artwork. That is the work, and the fellowship year is where I'd push it
from one person's system toward many people's public.
