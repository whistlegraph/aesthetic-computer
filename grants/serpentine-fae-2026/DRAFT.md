## Working title

**Held in Common — re-founding the personal computer as a creative commons**

## 1 · Practice & context
*(FAE: "clearly describe the applicant's practice and context")*

I am a painter and toolmaker. For six years I have been writing software and
patterning for **Aesthetic Computer** — a mobile-first runtime, operating
system, and social network for creative computing — and giving it away as a
commons. It is one system end to end: a kernel that boots refurbished laptops
straight into it, a piece API you program against in JavaScript or **KidLisp**
(a minimal Lisp built for it), the static media people make alongside those
pieces — paintings, tapes, chats — a publication channel
(papers.aesthetic.computer), and a daily public output. People make and run
their own work on it; they don't rent it. Roughly thirty papers, an open
codebase, and a small community (biweekly NELA Computer Club demos at
Plot.Place, Chinatown LA) come out of it. My tools *No Paint* and *notepat* have
each reached the front page of Hacker News. I am currently Artist in Residence
at UCLA's Social Software studio. The throughline is simple: I build the
instruments I make my own work with, in public, and hand them to other people.

## 2 · Research question / line of inquiry
*(FAE: "articulate a compelling research question, concern, or line of inquiry")*

**As computing converges on the data center, what would it take to re-found the
personal computer instead — as a creative commons a community owns and plays
together?**

Personal computing is being re-constituted in front of us. As demand for AI
compute concentrates everything into data centers, the personal computer is
becoming a thin client — a window onto someone else's machine, rented by the
month. The consumer OS already keeps your identity in a cloud account; this
finishes the job. The genuinely unstable category — the one I want to stay
inside rather than resolve early — is "the personal computer" itself: is it
yours, or a thin client? I think home computing needs a revolution at the OS
layer, and I've spent six years building one. The inquiry is whether a creative
OS can take language models in as **local, owned faculties** — making a personal
computer more capable — *without* becoming a tenant of the cloud, and whether a
room of surplus laptops, held in common, can be a real instrument the way the
Princeton Laptop Orchestra proved twenty years ago, only planetary and free.

## 3 · Connection to Art × Convergence
*(FAE: "explain connection to the Art × Convergence theme")*

Convergence, in your sense, is two systems that used to be held apart —
the personal computer and the data center — interpenetrating and remaking each
other, with agentic AI as the accelerant. Most artists meet that from the
outside: as users of hosted creative-AI services, or as critics of them. I want
to meet it from inside, by holding the whole stack — kernel, language, social
layer, papermill — and treating the substrate itself as the medium. That
reframes "convergence" from something that happens *to* artists into something
an artist and a community can *own a piece of*. Agentic AI is the force doing
the enclosing — but it's also the reason one artist and a small community can
now build and tend a whole OS that used to take a company. Aesthetic Computer is
already the long-form argument that artists can hold their own infrastructure
rather than renting it; the fellowship's R&D is the part where I push that into
the place convergence actually bites — the OS layer — and write down what
public value is created when the ground under art × tech is owned and legible
rather than platform-rented.

## 4 · Why this fellowship suits my development
*(FAE: "show why the Fellowship suits your development")*

**Documented process is the deliverable, and documenting in public is already
what AC does** — source, papers, daily editions, dossiers on every adjacent
organisation. The fellowship doesn't ask me to do something foreign; it gives me
six months and a serious room to push one hard thread — the OS layer, where
convergence bites — with the care it deserves, instead of squeezing it between
everything else. It is also the rare programme that funds *the practice of
building infrastructure* rather than *artworks that use it* — the right shape for
what AC actually is. And the panel makes the work self-explaining from two sides
at once: **Mariana Mazzucato, Venkatesh Rao and Lila Ibrahim** make the
public-value and artist-owned-infrastructure framing legible to people who think
about systems and economics for a living; **Holly Herndon, Refik Anadol and Cao
Fei** keep the artistic stakes of owning your own apparatus in the room.

**What the six months produces:**
- *Sep–Nov.* Harden AC Native OS into a daily-usable personal computer on
  surplus hardware: the boot-to-instrument path, identity in the device,
  over-the-air updates, and a clear written account of what a home computer is
  when it's owned, legible, and account-free.
- *Nov–Jan.* The riskiest thread — prototype letting agentic AI into the OS on
  its own terms, running locally where it can, governed by the commons, never
  phoning the data center. What can a model make a personal computer capable of
  without making it a tenant? This is what I most want the cohort to argue with.
- *Jan–Mar.* Grow the laptop orchestra off the campus — a shared language,
  shared instruments, surplus hardware, played together, starting with the NELA
  Computer Club in LA. Output: a paper, a public work-log, and the binary.

**On risk (stated plainly):** local AI on a $50 laptop is a real constraint, and
"where it can" is doing work in that sentence. If the models won't fit, the
finding — how far a personal computer carries on its own before it has to call
out — is worth publishing on its own.
