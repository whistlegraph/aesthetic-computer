## What the call is about

**Theme: Art × Convergence.** The brief frames "convergence" as the current
condition where AI systems can pursue goals, model environments, and act in
the world — and asks how that reshapes cultural and societal systems. The
fellowship is explicitly for **creative R&D rather than finished outcomes**;
documentation of process is the expected deliverable.

Eligible: artists, curators, technologists, and organisers working at the
intersection of art and advanced technologies. International applicants
welcome if they can attend the three London weekends.

## Why AC fits — one spine

**Re-founding the personal computer as a creative commons.** *(Chosen spine
2026-06-04 — supersedes the earlier voice / Pink Trombone framing; see
`feedback_proposals_single_spine.md` — one spine per venue.)*

The panel reads art + tech + *economics* hard (Mazzucato on public value, Rao
on systems, Ibrahim from DeepMind, Spiegel from Snap). The framing that lands
across that whole table is **artist-owned infrastructure at the OS layer**, not
a single instrument. The argument, lived in one system:

1. **The thin-client problem.** As AI compute concentrates into data centers,
   the personal computer is becoming a window onto someone else's machine,
   rented by the month. Identity already lives in a cloud account; the line
   between "my computer" and "the data center" is dissolving. That's
   convergence: two systems that used to be held apart, interpenetrating, with
   agentic AI as the accelerant.
2. **AC Native OS as the counter-move.** A bare-metal Linux that boots any
   surplus x86-64 laptop into a creative instrument in ~7s, in ~89MB, with no
   desktop, no account, no subscription. Identity lives in the boot partition;
   the machine greets you by name. A retired $50 ThinkPad becomes a personal
   computer owned outright.
3. **A commons + a laptop orchestra.** KidLisp (the tiny language the community
   writes in), pieces at URLs, a papermill — all open, all boot-from-USB. The
   Princeton Laptop Orchestra proved a room of networked laptops could be a real
   instrument but stayed locked in rich universities at ~$1,500/seat; AC drops
   that by two orders of magnitude, so the orchestra can be planetary and held
   in common.

The live R&D question — and the riskiest part — is whether a creative OS can
take language models in as **local, owned faculties** (running on the machine,
governed by the commons) rather than turning the computer into a tenant of the
cloud. AC is poised to answer because it already holds the whole stack: kernel,
language, community, archive.

This is also paper-able. The OS and laptop-orchestra papers are already public
(`papers/arxiv-os/`), and the six months would produce a written piece for FAE
on re-founding personal computing as a commons.

## Why this venue specifically

- **Future Art Ecosystems** as a programme has been the most serious public
  thinking about the *infrastructure* under art × tech (their published
  reports are the closest thing the field has to a shared bibliography on
  the question). AC is, end-to-end, infrastructure — the OS, the runtime,
  the piece API, the social layer, the papermill. A fellowship that funds
  *the practice of building infrastructure* rather than *artworks that use
  infrastructure* is the right shape for what AC actually is.
- **The economics-heavy panel** (Mazzucato, Rao, Ibrahim, Spiegel) makes the
  artist-owned-infrastructure / public-value framing land without translation;
  the artists (Herndon, Anadol, Cao Fei) keep the stakes of owning your own
  apparatus in the room. The OS/commons spine speaks to the whole table.
- **The London weekends** are tractable: three trips over six months from
  LA is a real commitment but not destabilising, and travel + accommodation
  are covered.

## What we would actually do across the six months

Three threads, paced to the London weekends (final shape in `FORM-ANSWERS.md`):

- **Sep–Nov.** Harden AC Native OS into a daily-usable personal computer on
  surplus hardware: boot-to-instrument path, identity in the device,
  over-the-air updates, and a written account of what a home computer is when
  it's owned, legible, and account-free. Measure: more people booting throwaway
  machines into the commons.
- **Nov–Jan.** The live, riskiest question — prototype letting agentic AI into
  the OS on its own terms, running locally where it can, governed by the
  commons, never phoning the data center. Local faculties, not landlords.
- **Jan–Mar.** Grow the laptop orchestra off the campus — shared language,
  shared instruments, surplus hardware, played together, starting with the NELA
  Computer Club in LA; harden KidLisp as a dependable standard. Output: a paper,
  a public work-log, the binary.

## Things the panel will likely look at hard

- **Public surface.** The fellowship rewards documented process. AC
  already publishes everything (papers.aesthetic.computer, the platter,
  daily aesthetic-24 recaps). That's a major fit signal.
- **Originality of the question.** "Re-found the personal computer as a commons"
  is unusual framing — most artists relate to data-center convergence as users
  or critics, not by holding the whole stack from kernel to community. Lean
  into that.
- **Realism of the plan.** Local AI on a $50 laptop is a real constraint;
  "where it can" is doing work in that sentence. Be honest about the limit and
  frame the finding — how far a personal computer carries before it has to call
  out — as publishable on its own.
