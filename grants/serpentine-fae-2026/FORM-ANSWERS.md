## Fellowship Proposal  *(500 words max — context · what's at stake & why now · connection to Art × Convergence)*

Personal computing is being re-constituted in front of us. As the demand for AI compute concentrates everything into data centers, the personal computer is becoming a thin client — a window onto someone else's machine, rented by the month. The consumer OS already keeps your identity in a cloud account; this finishes the job. The line between "my computer" and "the data center" is dissolving. That's convergence in the sense you mean: two systems that used to be held apart, interpenetrating and remaking each other, with agentic AI as the accelerant.

I think home computing needs a revolution at the OS layer. Not another app — a different ground to stand on. I've spent six years building one.

Aesthetic Computer is a creative-computing commons: a runtime, a tiny language the community writes in (KidLisp), a social layer, a papermill. And AC Native OS — a bare-metal Linux that boots any surplus x86-64 laptop into a creative instrument in 7.3 seconds, in 89 megabytes, with no desktop, no account, no subscription. Identity lives in the boot partition. The machine greets you by name. A retired $50 ThinkPad becomes a personal instrument you own outright.

It's also a laptop-orchestra argument. In 2005 the Princeton Laptop Orchestra proved a room of networked laptops could be a real instrument. But at fifteen hundred dollars a seat it stayed locked inside rich universities and reached maybe a thousand people a year. AC Native OS drops that cost by two orders of magnitude. On hardware the upgrade cycle throws away, that orchestra can be planetary — and held in common.

Here's what's at stake. If the personal computer becomes a terminal, the most basic site of creative autonomy — a machine you own and can read all the way down — gets enclosed. The unstable thing, the genuinely uncertain category, is "the personal computer" itself: is it yours, or a thin client? That's an art question as much as a technical one, and I'd rather stay inside it than resolve it early.

What I want to push on is the part LLMs change. Agentic AI is the force doing the enclosing — but it's also the reason one artist and a small community can now build and tend a whole OS that used to take a company. I already let agents into AC on its own terms: locally, governed by the commons, not routed through a data center. So: can a creative OS take language models in as local, owned faculties — making a personal computer more capable — without becoming a tenant of the cloud? I think AC is poised to answer that, because it already holds the whole stack, kernel to community.

That's the divergence. Branch away from the data-center default. Re-found the personal computer as a commons.

**Harden the OS into a real personal computer** *(Sep–Nov).* Make AC Native OS something you'd use every day on a surplus laptop: the boot-to-instrument path, identity in the device, over-the-air updates, and a clear account of what a home computer is when it's owned, legible, and account-free. **LLMs as local faculties, not landlords** *(Nov–Jan).* The live question, and the riskiest part. ## What has most influenced the trajectory of your practice or profession?  *(200 words max)*

Two things, pulling against each other.

Painting first. I trained as a painter — Ringling, then Yale — and never left it. The immediacy of a mark on a surface is still the standard everything else has to answer to. My digital work started as an attempt to make software feel that immediate. That became Radical Digital Painting: the paint program as a medium, not a utility, performed live.

Then the realisation that the tool is the work. Touring Europe performing drawing with Goodiepal & Pals, and years of teaching — UCLA, Parsons, Southern Oregon — convinced me that handing someone an instrument changes more than handing them an image. So I stopped renting my tools and built my own, in public. No Paint, then Aesthetic Computer.

The throughline is ownership and immediacy together: hold the whole stack, from the gesture to the kernel. What's changed lately is the stakes. Once other people make and keep their work on what you've built, it's a commons. And as computing gets pulled into the data center, holding onto a computer you actually own stops being a preference and becomes the thing worth defending.

## What do you want to enable for others through your practice?  *(200 words max)*

I want more people to own a computer they can actually read, and hold it in common — to treat the OS, the language, and the place their work lives as shared material they tend, not platforms they rent.

That's what Aesthetic Computer is for. KidLisp lets you write a generative piece in a few legible lines. The piece lives at a URL anyone can open. The whole thing is open and boots from a single file on a USB stick, so the cost of entry is basically zero — a $50 surplus laptop the upgrade cycle threw out. The Princeton Laptop Orchestra proved laptops could be a real ensemble, then stayed locked in rich universities. I want that planetary, and held in common.

What I most want to leave behind is durable independence. Not just that people can build their own substrate — that it lasts. That a community can depend on it. That a piece still runs in ten years. That the knowledge of how to keep it alive is written down and shared. I'd rather hand over a living commons and a method anyone can repeat than a sealed artwork. Agency that endures.
