## What they're calling for

Internet-art projects that take a clear-eyed look at the past while exploring how
internet histories can open paths to more equitable futures. Not "old internet"
nostalgia — but interrogation of platform power, data extraction, and the
infrastructures we've inherited. Asking what protocols, networks, archives, and
shared conditions are needed to build the internet we actually want.

## Eligibility

Open worldwide to:
- artists
- collectives
- curators
- arts initiatives
- exhibition spaces
- online platforms
- developers (software and hardware)

## Format of award

- Grant (sliding €1.5k–€7k)

- Inclusion in online exhibition (end of 2026)

## Why AC fits

- Aesthetic.Computer *is* a critique of platform-era computing through making.
- The URL Tradition paper (`papers/arxiv-url-tradition/`) is the clearest single
  artifact framing AC's stance on protocols and shared conditions for the web.
- The Library + AC Native OS '26 reframes "old hardware" as a site of cultural
  reuse rather than nostalgia — anti-Windows-10-EOL e-waste, against platform
  capture, in favor of artist-run infrastructure.
- KidLisp + the social network are the "protocols, networks, archives" the call
  asks about, made tangible.

## Suggested angle

**"Personal Computers Are Not Done Yet" → reframed for Netstalgia:**
We are not nostalgic for the early web. We are arguing the second personal
computing scene is just starting, and it has to be built on the lessons of why
the first one was captured. The AC Library and AC Native OS are the
demonstration: 240M stranded laptops, an artist-run lending fleet, a custom
kernel that boots straight into a creative instrument, no app store. Online
exhibition piece could be a browser-runnable demonstration of AC's URL tradition
+ a documentation reel from a Flash Day.
