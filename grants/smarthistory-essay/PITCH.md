## Email Body

Dear Smarthistory editors,

I'd like to propose an 800–1500 word object-based essay on Lillian
Schwartz's *Pixillation* (1970, 4 min, 16mm), a film-as-program made
at Bell Labs that I think still doesn't get a fair reading in
contemporary art history. I'd write it as a practitioner-historian — I
make programs that are themselves artworks, and I can walk a beginning
art-history reader through *Pixillation* as both an image and an
algorithm, with the goal of leaving them able to look at any
computer-generated artwork and ask better questions about it.

I notice your contributor guidelines lead with the PhD requirement and
make space for "appropriate qualifications outside traditional academic
disciplines." I'm an artist, programmer, and educator: MFA from Yale
School of Art (2013); currently Author in Residence at UCLA Social
Software with Casey Reas and Lauren Lee McCarthy; have taught at UCLA,
Parsons School of Design, and Southern Oregon University; my work is
in the collections of KADIST (San Francisco) and the SMK National
Gallery of Denmark; I gave the keynote at Korea HCI 2020 and India HCI
2019, and lectured at the New Museum in 2022 and at Chaos
Communication Congress in 2018. I've spent fifteen years writing,
teaching about, and making computer-generated art, and I'd be glad
to send writing samples on request.

If the editorial fit is right, happy to share a 500-word outline and a
draft for review. Thanks for considering it.

Jeffrey Alan Scudder
mail@aesthetic.computer
https://aesthetic.computer / https://kidlisp.com

## Object Candidates (pick one before sending)

1. **Lillian Schwartz, *Pixillation* (1970)** — algorithmic film at
   Bell Labs. Strong fit; under-discussed; lets the essay walk through
   image AND algorithm; situates Jeffrey's KidLisp lineage.
2. **John Whitney, *Catalog* (1961)** — analog computer animation; the
   foundational object for "computer-generated art." Bigger, more
   crowded, but the "what does the program do" walk-through is rich.
3. **Casey Reas, one of the *Process* series, e.g., *Process 7* (2005)**
   — contemporary, very accessible, but Reas is Jeffrey's current
   collaborator (UCLA Social Software), so there's a conflict-of-interest
   note to disclose.
4. **A specific KidLisp piece minted on Tezos** — most contemporary,
   most about JS's own lineage, but harder to sell as a "single object
   with art-historical weight" to a beginner audience.
5. **A Whistlegraph drawing** — same risk as #4, plus Smarthistory
   doesn't have a strong precedent for sound-image hybrids on a
   single page.

**Recommendation:** lead with **#1 (Schwartz, *Pixillation*)** — best
balance of art-historical heft, beginner-readability, and Jeffrey's
qualifications-equivalent angle. Hold #3 in reserve if the editor
suggests something contemporary.
