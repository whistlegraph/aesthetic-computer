THE STORY — "held down / breaking free"
=======================================

the cover's origin myth in thirty seconds. a giant three-headed singing
robot — cute, chibi, candy-color polychrome panels, roped butterfly
wings — is found by the tiny winged pixsies of the world, feared, lashed
down gulliver-style, and sings its way free. fear becomes love; the
crowd that held the ropes ends up riding its shoulders singing along.

  saturated pencil, streaming from singing mouths.

THE CUT — 6 WIDE BEATS (30.0s · vertical 1080×1920)
====================================================
1. found  (0-5)   night field. the robot lies dormant in tall grass,
                  curled like a sleeping toy, chest glowing faintly
                  through the panel seams. a handful of pixsies hover
                  close with little lanterns, wide-eyed, discovering it.
2. sing   (5-10)  the three heads wake and begin to SING — eyes opening,
                  first bright notes rising off their mouths into the
                  dark. the nearest pixsies startle backward mid-air.
3. ropes  (10-15) the frightened pixsie crowd swarms in with ropes and
                  stakes, lashing the robot down, roping the wings flat.
                  it keeps singing through the lashing, not angry.
4. strain (15-20) THE COVER FRAME — half-risen against dozens of taut
                  staked ropes, chest blazing red white and blue light
                  through the lashings, notes tangling in the lines,
                  pixsies hauling and dangling.
5. snap   (20-25) the ropes SNAP — lines whipping loose, stakes popping
                  from the soil, pixsies tumbling and catching air on
                  their own wings, the robot rising to full height,
                  butterfly wings spreading wide, notes everywhere.
6. free   (25-30) airborne over the field — the robot flying, pixsies
                  riding its shoulders and arms SINGING ALONG, the sky
                  full of notes and fireworks. nobody holds the ropes.
                  fear became the chorus.
