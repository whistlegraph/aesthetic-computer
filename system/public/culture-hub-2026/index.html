Project description~400 w · drafting

Aesthetic.Computer is a creative computing system that boots a Linux laptop directly into art software with no desktop and no browser. The proposed residency stages it as a bandstand: two AC Blank laptops on stage, a menuband overlay pinned to the top of each screen scoring every keystroke, notepat playing the actual notes, and an open invitation for the audience to sit down and write a piece in KidLisp. CultureHub's networked-performance infrastructure connects the room to a remote AC station — La MaMa to a UCLA Social Software session, or to NELA Computer Club at Plot.Place — for cross-site jam sessions where each side plays into the same composition over UDP MIDI.

§Pitch

Aesthetic.Computer is a creative computing system: a Linux kernel that boots a custom C runtime as PID 1 on x86_64 UEFI laptops, with no desktop, no compositor, no browser. It exists because the personal computer, after forty years of platform consolidation, stopped being a personal instrument. The residency at CultureHub puts the instrument back on a stage.

menuband — the conductor sits in the menu bar.

A small Swift app in slab/menuband/ pins a glass status-bar above whatever the artist is doing. It listens to keys the way a band listens to a count-off: deliberate-only triggers, dark-mode piano theme, family-color shadow on the title. At the residency, menuband sits at the top of every screen on the stage and on every screen in the audience. When a visitor presses a key, it appears as a piano-roll note above the editor — visible, public, scored. It turns a laptop into a sitting-down instrument with a transparent score.

AC Native — the OS as ensemble member.

The two laptops on stage are AC Blanks: refurbished ThinkPad 11e Yoga Gen 6s flashed with Aesthetic.Computer at $128/seat. AC Native renders graphics through DRM without a compositor, reads input from raw evdev streams, and synthesizes audio sample-by-sample through ALSA at 192 kHz with 32-voice polyphony. There is a built-in code command that drops into a native terminal running an AI coding partner — so the residency can also explore improvisation between an artist and a model, on stage, inside the same OS.

notepat — the actual instrument.

The default piece on the AC Blank is notepat: an 8,466-line polyphonic instrument with eight waveforms (sine, triangle, sawtooth, square, composite, harp, whistle, sample), 12 simultaneous sample voices, USB and UDP MIDI in/out. The keyboard is the keys; the menuband is the score; the speakers are the band. UDP MIDI is what makes the residency networked: two AC stations across the internet can hear each other's notes inside ~50 ms over geckos.io, so a player at La MaMa and a player at UCLA Social Software can hold the same chord.

KidLisp — the audience writes the score.

KidLisp is a minimal Lisp dialect for generative art: 118 built-in functions, no build step, no imports, accessible to non-programmers. There are already 17,000+ KidLisp programs on the platform, written by 2,800+ handles. At the residency, a public KidLisp Jam opens the bandstand to the audience: five lines, a permanent URL, and the running composition picks up the new piece. A jam is what happens when a score is written in the same room as the music.

Cross-site — La MaMa to LA, every night.

CultureHub's networked-performance studio is the only reason this proposal exists. The residency stages a two-room composition: one AC station at La MaMa, one at UCLA Social Software (or NELA Computer Club at Plot.Place), bridged over UDP MIDI. Both rooms run the same OS, the same instrument, the same menuband. The audience at La MaMa can see — projected on the back wall — the menuband from the other room, scoring the keys of a player they cannot see. The two rooms hold one chord.

Learning Aesthetic.Computer — Flash Day on Saturday.

The residency closes its public weekend with a Flash Day: ten participants are walked through flashing a USB stick that turns any UEFI laptop into the same instrument they just heard played. Each participant leaves with the OS, the editor, the menuband, notepat, and a KidLisp reference card set. The instrument is contagious by design.

This is the live edge of an argument the studio has been making elsewhere: the personal computer is an unfinished instrument, and performance is part of how it gets finished. CultureHub is where the instrument gets to play to a room with a partner across the country.

§The Lineagespecific, not canonical

PLOrk · Princeton
The original laptop orchestra (2005–). $1,500+/seat hand-fabricated. The artistic question was settled in year one; what failed was access. AC's per-seat cost lands ~$50 — two orders of magnitude below — and is the substance of papers/arxiv-plork.
Goodiepal
Radical Computer Music. A parallel argument that the computer itself is under-designed as an artistic instrument. I toured Europe with him in 2018 — the residency descends from that tour.
CultureHub · Re-Fest lineage
Networked-performance work staged across rooms — exactly the substrate this proposal is designed for. The bandstand idea is ours; the network it crosses is yours.
SuperCollider / Max
Real-time sample-level synthesis as a composable medium. AC's audio ring buffer at 192 kHz is downstream of this tradition, reimagined as a piece's native voice rather than a separate DAW.
HyperCard · Bill Atkinson
The last mainstream system where anyone on a computer could author a complete interactive thing. AC reconstructs that social contract for 2026; KidLisp is the script language; the URL is the stack.
Cornelius Cardew · Scratch Orchestra
An artist-led collective attempt to build the system that would perform their scores. AC is this pattern in software; the residency stages it for one weekend at La MaMa.
