§Pitch

The fair celebrates the printed thing. Aesthetic Computer's publishing practice is unusual — the printed card and the live software are the same artifact. A KidLisp piece has a permanent URL the moment it is written; the printed card carries that URL as a QR code; scanning the card re-runs the piece. The card is the edition; the URL is the run.

For four years the project has run a quiet papermill alongside the software. Thirty arxiv-format papers. A bound AC Reader. A dis-order pamphlet. A growing set of single-sheet KidLisp cards designed for Casey Reas and Lauren Lee McCarthy's Social Software course at UCLA. The mill exists to press the thinking inside Aesthetic Computer into forms that can leave the building. The Project Space is the live edge of that mill.

menuband — performing the keys.

Above the booth's two laptops a small status-bar overlay called menuband runs across the top of the screen. It is a working AC subsystem: a pinned glass panel that turns whatever the player types into a piano-roll of the notes pressed, with a deliberate-only trigger (no accidental toots) and a dark-mode piano theme. At the fair it does two jobs: it makes the act of writing a piece visible to the viewer behind the visitor, and it scores the keystrokes back as music — the piece prints, but it also plays.

AC Native — the OS the page came from.

The two laptops at the booth are AC Blanks — refurbished ThinkPad 11e Yoga Gen 6s flashed with Aesthetic.Computer at $128/seat. AC Native is a Linux kernel that boots a custom C runtime as PID 1 directly into the editor: no desktop, no compositor, no browser. 32-voice audio at 192 kHz, raw evdev input, USB+UDP MIDI, a built-in code command for an AI coding partner inside the OS. The visitor at the booth never sees a window manager. They see the editor, the menuband across the top, and the printer next to the keyboard. The OS is the press.

Performing music — the booth as a small instrument.

Visitors compose at notepat, AC's default polyphonic instrument (8,466 lines: eight waveforms, sample recording, USB+UDP MIDI). At the booth, notepat is wired to a small portable speaker so what you write is heard by the visitor next to you. Twice a day the artist runs a scheduled "press concert": a 6-minute live KidLisp + notepat performance from the booth, after which the day's pieces are bound into a 16-page newsprint zine and stacked on the wall.

Learning Aesthetic.Computer — the curriculum is on the wall.

Behind the booth, the wall holds the curriculum used in UCLA Social Software (Spring 2026): KidLisp reference cards, lesson sheets, and a pinned poster that takes a brand-new visitor from "no code" to "your piece is on the wall and on the internet" in fifteen minutes. The teaching layer is honest: the booth's whole point is that anyone walking in the door can publish before they leave.

This is the printed-matter version of an argument the studio has been making elsewhere: the personal computer is an unfinished instrument, and publishing is part of how it gets finished. A Project Space lets us run the press in front of the readers.

§Four Registers

Card

Single-sheet, monochrome, 2.75″ × 4.75″. Pixel render at top, full source mid, QR to the live URL at bottom. The piece fits in your hand.

Reader

One bound volume (papers/reader.pdf) collecting all current AC arxiv papers. Free copy per visitor while supply lasts. Translated EN / DA / ES / ZH.

Zine

A 16-page newsprint zine bound twice a day from that morning's KidLisp pieces. Edition of 50–100. Stack stays on the wall, refilled from the press.

Wall

The curriculum used in UCLA Social Software (Spring 2026): reference cards, lesson sheets, posters. The visitor borrows the syllabus on the way out.
