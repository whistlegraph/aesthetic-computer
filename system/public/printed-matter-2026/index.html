§Pitch

For five years, Aesthetic Computer has run a small artist-publishing press on the side of its software practice. The papermill has put out a bound Reader, a wall's worth of single-author papers, fourteen organizational dossiers, sets of cards, and a steady drift of pamphlets. It is run by one person, on a laptop, in public most of the time but never at scale. NYABF is where it gets to operate at scale, in front of an audience that already cares about printed matter.

A working press, in the room, for four days.

The booth has two halves. On one side, a wall: five years of accumulated pages, free for visitors to take. On the other, a workstation: the press itself, still running. Each day during the fair, the artist sits down and works on whatever is currently in production — a paper being typeset, a cover being generated, a dossier being drafted, a pamphlet being laid out, a Reader being bound. That day's output goes up on the wall. By Sunday, half the wall is the past five years and half is the four days at PS1.

What the visitor sees.

A wall of pages, a small workstation, an artist working. Visitors browse the wall, pick what catches their eye, take it home. They can stand and watch a page get made. They can ask the artist what they're working on. They leave with paper in their hands and, if they want, the day's fresh page on top.

The booth is quiet on purpose. No theatre, no concerts, no projection. The press makes a small steady amount of noise — a fan, a printer, the artist typing.

Why this works as a Project Space.

NYABF Project Spaces frame a publication or printed-matter practice rather than just selling books from a table. Aesthetic Computer's printed matter is the literal output of a small running press, and what the Project Space puts in front of visitors is not finished books but the press itself, still running. The cultural object is the operation: five years of work made physically present, and a fifth and final day of work still to come.

§A Day at the Presswhat gets made each day · drawn from real production

The press isn't asked to perform; it does what it normally does. Four examples of plausible Project-Space-day outputs, each one a real kind of thing the papermill makes.

Day 1 · A new paper, typeset
An in-progress paper draft moves from prose to typeset PDF in the room: cover pulled from the figures pipeline, references resolved, footnotes cross-checked, layout tightened, proof printed. The day ends with a single freshly-printed copy on the wall, alongside the previous forty-four.
Day 2 · A new dossier page
An organizational dossier gets a new section drafted in public: sources read, structured data table updated, a paragraph rewritten, the page exported and pinned. Visitors see the difference between writing about an institution and citing it, in real time.
Day 3 · A pamphlet, folded
A short single-sheet pamphlet is laid out, printed, and folded by hand at the table. Topic chosen from current AC concerns. Copies accumulate on the wall and on the takeaway stack until they're gone.
Day 4 · A fresh Reader
A new edition of the bound AC Reader is compiled with the four days' new pages folded in, exported as a single PDF, printed, and bound at the booth as the closing act of the fair. The first copy goes up on the wall; subsequent copies are taken home.

§The Booth, in Four Parts

The wall

Five years of Aesthetic Computer's printed work — Reader, papers, dossiers, cards, pamphlets — pinned up and free to take. Refilled each morning.

The press

One laptop, one small printer, one artist sitting at a small table doing the work the papermill normally does in private.

The new pages

Each day's output is added to the wall. By the close of the fair, the wall holds both archive and edition: the past five years and the four days at PS1.

The host

The artist runs the booth solo and is in conversation, not selling. Visitors can browse, watch, ask, take. Nothing is for sale.
