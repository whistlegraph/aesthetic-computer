§Pitch

I want to write about whistlegraph — a drawing form I invented in 2019, where every line is a sung syllable of a poem. You draw it while you sing it. The finished drawing is a score. The score teaches you how to play it.

Between 2019 and 2023, a trio — me, Alex Freundlich, and Camille Klein — scaled the form from a cabin in Ashland, Oregon to 2.6 million followers on TikTok. No paid promotion. No trend-jacking. No dance. The early viral pieces were four strokes, four sung lines — kids learned them faster than most kids learn Twinkle, Twinkle.

I want to write about why it worked there.

The graphic-score tradition failed at one thing.

Cardew's Treatise is 193 pages of beautiful, unreadable geometry. Cage's Fontana Mix needs a transparent overlay. Riley's In C wants 53 phrases and an ensemble. The 20th-century graphic score succeeded as art — Notations, MoMA, 1969 — and failed as communication. The premise was that notation could be anything. The consequence was that performance required a trained interpreter.

Whistlegraph takes the same ingredients and swaps one assumption: the relationship between mark and sound is one-to-one, fixed during the first performance and preserved in the recording. No interpretation. No ambiguity. A child who can hold a pen can perform one after watching it once.

That is the actual innovation, and it is the reason a drawing went viral on a platform designed for lip-sync. A dance challenge works because the choreography is the score; a whistlegraph works for the same reason, except the score is also original composition, also the artwork, also the instruction manual. Papert would have called it a microworld. Eshun's sonic fiction has a visual analogue here: a drawing that constructs the performance it depicts.

Three registers, one object.

As art, whistlegraph lives downstream of Cardew, Cage, and the Fluxus event score. Rhizome / New Museum commissioned a 22-minute chalk whistlegraph in 2022; Feral File sold 45 editions the same year; SMK, KADIST, and the Schneider Museum hold work in collection. As content, it broke the viral-mechanics assumption that TikTok only rewards bodies and pop audio. As interface design, it is the founding principle of aesthetic.computer — every piece a self-documenting score, every URL a memorizable performance.

The trio separated in November 2023. The form did not. What is left is the claim: reproducibility, not novelty, is the real score of a form. The essay is about what it would mean to design more things this way — objects whose instructions and performance are the same object.

Paper Rad co-founder. Wrote The Butterfly Effect / Rules Set You Free for the 2023 Whistlegraph Zine (ed. Asher Penn, Sex Magazine, 750 copies) — the essay that placed the form in the rule-based community-art lineage.
Cornelius Cardew
Scratch Orchestra and Nature Study Notes — not just Treatise. The group was the attempt to solve the interpretation problem. Whistlegraph attacks the same problem from the other side: remove interpretation from the score.
Pauline Oliveros
Sonic Meditations. Attention as performance. Whistlegraph asks the same of the viewer: watch, learn, reproduce.
Seymour Papert
Mindstorms (1980) — microworlds. A whistlegraph is a microworld: constrained, memorizable, constructible by the viewer.
Kodwo Eshun
More Brilliant Than the Sun (1998) — sonic fiction. Whistlegraph is its visual analogue: a drawing that constructs the performance it depicts.
Paul Klee
Pedagogical Sketchbook (1925) — "the line that goes for a walk." Whistlegraph's lines take walks while singing.
Olia Lialina / JODI
Rhizome ArtBase neighbours. Whistlegraph sits in the same collection as early net art — the precedent for TikTok-native work entering an institutional archive.
Jalaiah Harmon

§Submission-Form Version~200 words

Whistlegraph and the Self-Teaching Score. I want to write about whistlegraph — a drawing form I invented in 2019, where every line is a sung syllable. Between 2020 and 2023, a trio I was in scaled it to 2.6 million TikTok followers from a cabin in Ashland, Oregon. No paid promotion. The distribution model was the form itself.

The 20th-century graphic-score tradition — Cardew, Cage, Brown, Feldman — is an art-historical success and a communication failure. Notation could be anything, so performance required a trained interpreter. Whistlegraph keeps the graphic score and removes the interpretation: mark-to-sound is one-to-one, fixed at performance time. A child who can hold a pen can reproduce it.

The essay moves through three registers. As art — downstream of Cardew, Ciocci, and Goodiepal, but for anyone. As content — the first viral graphic score. As interface design — the founding principle of aesthetic.computer.

What I want to work out: why reproducibility, not novelty, is the real score of a form, and what it would mean to design more objects this way — instructions and performance as the same object.
