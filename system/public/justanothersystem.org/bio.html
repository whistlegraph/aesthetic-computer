Jeffrey Alan Scudder

b. 1989, Assonet, MA
aesthetic.computer · jas.life · whistlegraph · @whistlegraph
I. Origins

Assonet to Arena

Jeffrey Alan Scudder grew up in Assonet, Massachusetts — a small village in the town of Freetown, between Fall River and New Bedford. Before he ever held a paintbrush, he held a controller. As a teenager he competed in Unreal Tournament 2004 under the handle "verdeo," winning the New Jersey qualifier for the World Cyber Games 2004 and traveling to the USA National Finals at GameWorks in Long Beach, California.

Kid Pix was formative. Years later, he would track down its creator, Craig Hickman, and make a short film about the encounter.

Meeting Mr. Kid Pix (2019)
II. Jeffrey Heart

The Performance Identity

At Ringling College of Art and Design (BFA Fine Art, 2011), Scudder created an artistic alter ego called Jeffrey Heart — a performance art identity with its own email address, ceremonially birthed in 2008 and killed in 2010. Under this name he drew compulsively, kept confessional diaries, formed a dorm-room band called LOVE HEART EVERYTHING, and produced handmade books of drawings: HEART ATTACK, THE HISTORY OF HEART, Jeffrey's Girlfriend.

The writing from this period — preserved in an archive of 109 GB of projects — reads like a hybrid of Frank O'Hara's lunch poems and early alt-lit: observational, diaristic, self-aware. His notebooks from the New York Studio Program residency are titled things like "TALK-TO-STRANGERS-CAMPAIGN" and "Future-is-Dead-Silent." A portrait of Tao Lin sits in the archive alongside 612 encrypted email correspondences from jeffreyheart@gmail.com.

The Rhizome artist profile still credits him as "Jeffrey Alan Scudder (Jeffrey Heart)." The identity was the first of many systems he would build and then dismantle.

III. Sculpture as Software

Yale and the First Instruments

At Yale School of Art (MFA Sculpture, 2013), Scudder began building his own drawing software — treating code as material the way a sculptor treats clay. The result was a series of homegrown instruments that would define his practice: tools with unusual constraints, where the interface is the artwork.

With ThinkPad and Aesthetic Computer source code
@jeffrey — 596 handles set

After Yale he moved to New York, teaching at Parsons School of Design (Digital Painting, Core Lab: Interaction, Web Advanced: JavaScript) while developing his first public software works. No Paint (2014) is a constraint-based drawing application that removes the undo button — every mark is permanent. The console poems (Xbox Poem, Gamecube Poem, Team Scrub Poem) from 2016 were his first video works, treating game hardware as sculptural material.

Xbox Poem (2016)
IV. Radical Digital Painting

Painting is Just a Kind of Picture Message

In 2017, Scudder published A Manifesto for Radical Digital Painting during a web residency at Akademie Schloss Solitude. The manifesto proposed that digital tools are not supplements to painting but its next material ground. Over the following two years he gave more than 65 improvisational lecture performances at institutions across the US and Europe — Yale, Harvard, RISD, Cooper Union, UCLA, Bauhaus University Weimar, the Royal Swedish Academy, ZKM Karlsruhe, and many more.

"Painting is just a kind of picture message."

Each lecture was itself an artwork: Scudder would draw with chalk on gallery walls while projecting his software, improvising a live argument about the future of painting. In 2018 he received the Hash Award at ZKM Karlsruhe, presented at the 35th Chaos Communication Congress to an audience of 3,000 hackers, and showed Radical Digital Painting with Julia Yerger at Johannes Vogt Gallery in New York.

Radical Digital Painting at 35c3, Chaos Communication Congress (2018)

The Radical Digital Painting collection — 239 pictures painted between 2016 and 2021 using his own software — is held in part by KADIST (San Francisco) and SMK, the National Gallery of Denmark. The European tour with Goodiepal & Pals in 2018, mixing painting lectures with political rock performances, is documented in a feature-length road diary.

Radical Digital Painting & Political Rock Euro Tour (2018)
V. Whistlegraph

Drawing You Can Sing

In 2019, while teaching as Assistant Professor of Emerging Digital Practices at Southern Oregon University, Scudder invented the Whistlegraph — a hybrid art form combining drawing, singing, and poetry into reproducible graphic scores. A Whistlegraph is a memorizable procedure: three performers draw simultaneously while singing instructions, producing a poetic image through the performance of a score. It's human-executable code.

In 2020, the Whistlegraph trio formed with Alex Freundlich and Camille Klein. Their TikTok account, @whistlegraph, has reached over 2.7 million followers through musical drawing tutorials and live lecture-performances. In 2022, Rhizome commissioned the group for a performance at the New Museum, and Feral File exhibited Ten Whistlegraphs as digital editions.

VI. Aesthetic Computer

Building the Instrument

Since 2022, Scudder's central project has been Aesthetic Computer — a mobile-first runtime and social network for creative computing. It's designed like a musical instrument: users discover memorizable paths through commands and published "pieces" (interactive programs). Every tool he has built — the drawing apps, the painting instruments, the performance scores — converges here into a single platform.

Aesthetic Computer, sticker side
Building at night

The platform includes KidLisp, a minimal programming language for generative art with 118 built-in functions across 12 categories, and a growing archive of community pieces. An archive of 3,996 drawings is catalogued at Jeffrey's Drawings. On justanothersystem.org, he has returned to physical painting — stretched canvases and linen, made with the same attention to material constraint that defines his software.

VII. Trajectory

Where This Goes

Scudder is an artist who keeps rebuilding his own foundation. He created an identity and killed it. He made a painting manifesto and then built the instruments to realize it. He invented a performance medium and grew it to millions. Now he's building the operating system for all of it.

The through-line across every phase — Jeffrey Heart's handmade books, the Radical Digital Painting lectures, Whistlegraph, Aesthetic Computer — is a conviction that the tools we use to make pictures shape the pictures we can imagine. Rather than accepting the tools that exist, Scudder builds new ones from scratch, then teaches other people to use them. His work proposes that the most radical thing a painter can do in the 21st century is write their own software.

What distinguishes Scudder from other artist-programmers is that his software is not a means to an end — it is the work. No Paint removes undo. KidLisp fits on a napkin. Whistlegraph runs on three human bodies. Aesthetic Computer loads in a browser. Each system imposes a constraint that generates a new kind of picture. The constraint is the art.

He is heading toward a unified creative instrument — a system where drawing, painting, music, poetry, programming, teaching, and performance all happen in the same place, using the same language, on the same screen. Aesthetic Computer is not a portfolio site or a gallery. It's the thing itself: the studio, the canvas, the stage, the classroom, and the social network, compressed into a single URL.
