A single thick, hand-bound score book lying closed at a slight three-quarter angle, its covers cut from rough gray cardboard with a softly furred, worn edge and a fat block of crammed pages bulging between them. The whole thing looks like a homemade manifesto-manual: corners bumped and rounded from handling, a couple of pages dog-eared and poking out, a frayed rubber band slipped around the middle to hold it shut. The cover bears only a faint photocopied Courier paragraph and one rough Comic-Sans line, smudged and toner-streaked, with no readable title. It reads as a radical, unscannable artifact — a score addressed to a machine that does not exist yet — rendered in muted graphite gray, oatmeal cardboard tan, and a single faded teal toner streak.
