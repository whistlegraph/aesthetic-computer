\begin{bestguess}
\textbf{Buy nothing today.} Finish the 48-hour proof on the Xbox already in the
room. If native BIOS 1.0.0.11 remains stable, add at most
\textbf{two Xbox Series S 512GB consoles} and one
\textbf{TP-Link TL-SG108 eight-port gigabit switch}. That produces the largest
ordinary retail-Dev-Mode fleet allowed by Microsoft's published activation
terms: three active consoles total for an individual or company, affiliates
included. Use the Xboxes as synchronized native Aesthetic Computer screens and
independent job workers. Keep video generation on a CUDA/AI workstation; an
Xbox fleet does not pool memory into one larger GPU.
\end{bestguess}

\begin{gearwarning}[THE THREE-CONSOLE LINE]
Microsoft's retail Developer Mode activation agreement limits the stated test
and demonstration purpose to three consoles active at one time for an
individual or company, including affiliates. Do not buy a fourth active worker
under the assumption that retail Dev Mode is an unlimited lab license. A larger
fleet needs a different written program path from Microsoft, not a technical
workaround.
\end{gearwarning}

\section{why Series S wins the screen job}

\begin{gearcard}{same native surface / lower acquisition cost}
Series S supplies the same x64 UWP, Direct3D 11, XAudio2,
Windows.Gaming.Input, DirectWrite, HTTPS, and Device Portal shape now proven by
AC Native BIOS. Microsoft's hardware sheet lists an 8-core custom Zen 2 CPU, a
4-TFLOPS / 20-CU RDNA 2 GPU, 10GB physical GDDR6, 512GB NVMe storage, and
gigabit Ethernet. A display worker does not need the Series X disc drive or 4K
game target.
\end{gearcard}

\begin{gearcard}{the app budget matters more than the box number}
Retail Dev Mode runs the current BIOS as a UWP application. The archived
Microsoft Xbox resource table puts app-class work around 1GB of memory and
45\% of the GPU, while expanded game-class resources follow a restricted and
deprecated path. BIOS 1.0.0.11 therefore displays live
\texttt{MemoryManager} usage, limit, and expected limit on the console itself.
Use those measured values for purchasing decisions; do not count all 10GB or
16GB printed on the retail box as model memory.
\end{gearcard}

\begin{gearwarning}[WHEN SERIES X EARNS ITS PRICE]
Buy Series X only if the same packaged DirectML benchmark, same OS build, and
same app resource class demonstrate a useful wall-clock gain on a real target
model. Its 12-TFLOPS / 52-CU GPU and 16GB physical memory are impressive game
specifications, but they do not prove a larger UWP allocation. At today's
prices, two new Series X digital consoles cost \$1,199.98 versus \$799.98 for
two Series S units.
\end{gearwarning}

\section{tailnet shape}

The console cannot presently be treated as a supported first-class Tailscale
node. Put the route on the always-on \texttt{blueberry} controller instead:

\begin{gearcard}{one narrow route per console}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Reserve wired DHCP addresses, initially
        \texttt{192.168.1.101} through \texttt{.103}.
  \item On \texttt{blueberry}, advertise only those individual \texttt{/32}
        routes---not the entire home \texttt{/24}.
  \item Approve the routes in the tailnet and grant the deployment identity
        only TCP 11443 to those addresses.
  \item Keep Device Portal credentials in the controller vault. Never copy
        them to a console job or repository.
  \item Never port-forward Device Portal and never place it behind Funnel.
\end{enumerate}
\end{gearcard}

The Xboxes remain ordinary LAN devices behind a Tailscale subnet router; only
\texttt{blueberry} appears as a tailnet node. That is a feature for this fleet:
one controller owns authentication, package install, live source publication,
health polling, and the job ledger.

\section{what jobs actually distribute}

\begin{tabularx}{\linewidth}{@{}p{0.29\linewidth}Y p{0.18\linewidth}@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{job} & \textbf{fleet shape} & \textbf{verdict} \\
\midrule
Synchronized AC pieces & Broadcast one immutable piece/version; each screen renders locally. & excellent \\
Independent image seeds & Send one seed or small frame job per console; collect outputs centrally. & plausible after benchmark \\
Tiny vision / style models & ONNX Runtime DirectML on bounded tensors; measure allocation and operators. & plausible \\
Model-parallel diffusion & Split one model across console memory over gigabit Ethernet. & poor \\
Modern video generation & Multi-GB weights plus attention, activations, VAE, and frame state exceed app-class headroom. & workstation job \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}

ONNX Runtime's DirectML provider gives the honest experimental route for small
models. It requires DirectX 12-capable hardware and does not support parallel
execution on one inference session. That still fits a fan-out controller:
independent sessions, independent seeds, immutable inputs, results returned to
the shelf.

Modern open video models are the wrong first workload. Wan 2.1's smallest
published model still documents roughly 8.19GB VRAM and minutes for a five
second clip on an RTX 4090. LTX-Video's 2B FP16 weights occupy roughly 4GB
before runtime state; Mochi 1 is commonly documented around 60GB VRAM. No
arrangement of four small UWP allocations makes one shared 16GB or 60GB
device. If video generation becomes the requirement, price a CUDA workstation
or the 128GB DGX Spark as a separate compute shelf and let the Xboxes request,
display, animate, and sonify its results.

\section{day-one score}

\begin{gearcard}{proof gate / current Xbox}
\gearcheck Run Native BIOS 1.0.0.11 for 48 hours on wired Ethernet.\par
\gearcheck Record the exact Xbox model, OS build, live UWP memory limit,
controller inventory, feed refreshes, painting decodes, and oscillator state.\par
\gearcheck Publish and rollback at least twenty live pieces through Device
Portal without restarting the package.\par
\gearcheck Leave the rotating cube and paint counters visible as the living
render proof; capture one telemetry bundle after the run.\par
\gearcheck Fetch and render \texttt{\$obk} natively once the bounded KidLisp
closure is ready; no WebView success may satisfy this line.
\end{gearcard}

\begin{gearcard}{three-screen fleet / only after the gate}
\gearcheck Buy two matching Series S 512GB units, preferably the same hardware
revision and seller return window.\par
\gearcheck Label consoles \texttt{xbox-01} through \texttt{xbox-03}; label both
ends of every HDMI, power, and Ethernet lead.\par
\gearcheck Reserve \texttt{.101--.103}; vault each Device Portal identity by
device; never reuse a password outside this fleet.\par
\gearcheck Put immutable package hash, live-piece hash, temperature/power
observation, last heartbeat, and current job in one controller ledger.\par
\gearcheck Test loss: unplug one worker during a fan-out. The controller must
requeue only that worker's independent job and the other screens must continue.
\end{gearcard}

\begin{bestguess}[WHAT WOULD CHANGE THE GUESS]
A written Microsoft path above three active retail consoles would reopen the
four- or five-screen question. A measured Series X advantage under the same UWP
allocation would justify mixed hardware. A proven DirectML piece whose weights,
activations, and output fit below 70\% of the measured memory limit would earn a
small-model worker queue. None of those turns Xboxes into a pooled video GPU.
Until evidence changes, buy zero now, then two Series S units after the proof.
\end{bestguess}
