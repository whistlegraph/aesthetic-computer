\begin{abstract}
\noindent
\gla{} (GLA) reframes linear algebra as a diagrammatic language: linear
maps and relations become \emph{string diagrams}---generators wired
together, composed in sequence and in parallel---and proofs become visual
rewrites. Its pedagogy and its research artifacts, however, are
overwhelmingly \emph{static}: PDFs, slides, chalk. This paper argues that
Aesthetic Computer (AC) and its embedded language KidLisp are a natural
\emph{interactive} substrate for GLA. I sketch three concrete
bridges---a diagram renderer, an executable term language for props, and
an animated signal-flow playground---note honestly where the tree/graph
mismatch makes the problem interesting rather than trivial, and close with
a proposal for collaboration with the GLA / Applied Category Theory (ACT)
community.
\end{abstract}

\section{What Graphical Linear Algebra is}

GLA is the research program of Filippo Bonchi, Pawe\l{} Soboci\'nski, and
Fabio Zanasi (with Robin Piedeleu and others), popularised through
Soboci\'nski's long-running blog \emph{graphicallinearalgebra.net}. Its
core move is to stop writing linear algebra in matrices and symbols and
start \emph{drawing} it.

A diagram is built from a tiny set of \textbf{generators}---addition, the
constant zero, copy, discard, and multiplication-by-a-scalar---drawn as
boxes with wires. Two composition operators glue them:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{sequential} ($\circ$): connect the outputs of one diagram
    to the inputs of the next, left to right;
  \item \textbf{parallel} ($\otimes$): stack two diagrams side by side.
\end{itemize}

The algebra of these diagrams is captured by the symmetric monoidal
theory of \textbf{Interacting Hopf Algebras (IHA)}, a complete
presentation of the \emph{prop of linear relations}---relations that are
also linear subspaces. ``Complete'' is the punchline: two diagrams denote
the same linear relation \textbf{if and only if} one can be rewritten into
the other by the IHA equations. You no longer \emph{calculate}; you
\emph{redraw}. A headline application is \textbf{signal flow
graphs}---the dataflow diagrams engineers already use for linear
dynamical systems and circuits---which GLA gives a fully compositional
semantics.

The key conceptual inversion: in GLA the picture is not an
\emph{illustration} of the mathematics. The picture \textbf{is} the
mathematics.

\section{What Aesthetic Computer and KidLisp are}

Aesthetic Computer is a mobile-first runtime and social network for
creative computing: a musical-instrument-like interface where users
discover memorisable paths and run published ``pieces''---small
interactive programs addressed by URL. \textbf{KidLisp} is its embedded
Lisp dialect for generative art: S-expressions over ${\sim}118$ graphics,
timing, and math primitives, hot-reloaded live in the browser, every piece
shareable by a short link.

Two properties of AC matter for what follows:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{It is live and shareable by default.} A piece is a URL.
    There is no build step between an idea and a running, linkable
    artifact.
  \item \textbf{It treats spatial/visual interfaces as carriers of real
    computational meaning}---the same bet GLA makes about diagrams, aimed
    at a different domain.
\end{enumerate}

\section{The bridges}

\subsection{A diagram renderer (the easy, immediately useful one)}

A string diagram is a planar wiring of boxes: sequential composition runs
left-to-right, parallel composition stacks vertically. KidLisp already
draws boxes, lines, and text. A renderer that lays out GLA generators and
their wires is a short piece---the companion artifact \texttt{gla.lisp}
is a first, hand-placed instance: two inputs feeding an \textbf{add}
node, whose sum feeds a \textbf{copy} node that fans out to two outputs,
with animated dots showing the signal propagate. It runs in the browser
today.

The value here is pedagogical and immediate: GLA's teaching materials are
static; an embeddable, linkable, \emph{animated} diagram is not.

\subsection{An executable term language for props (the interesting one)}

The naive pitch---``Lisp is already a diagram language''---undersells the
problem, and saying so honestly is the whole point.

\textbf{Lisp S-expressions are trees. String diagrams are graphs.} A tree
has exactly one root and no sharing; an expression \texttt{(f (g x) (h
x))} \emph{names} \texttt{x} twice but the term structure is still a tree
with variables doing the sharing. GLA's power lives precisely in the
structure trees cannot express directly:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{copy / fan-out}---one wire becoming two---is sharing made
    first-class, \emph{without} a variable to name it;
  \item \textbf{discard}---throwing a wire away---is structural weakening;
  \item \textbf{feedback loops}---a signal flow graph's defining
    feature---are cycles, which a term tree simply does not have.
\end{itemize}

So ``render Lisp as diagrams'' is not the task. The real task is to design
a \textbf{textual syntax for a prop}---a term language whose denotation is
a morphism in a symmetric monoidal category, with explicit \texttt{copy},
\texttt{discard}, sequential $\circ$, and parallel $\otimes$ combinators,
plus a trace / feedback operator. This is a known and respected problem
(the \emph{internal language of monoidal categories}; related to the
algebra of Frobenius/Hopf structures and to hypergraph categories). A
KidLisp-flavored surface syntax that \textbf{evaluates} to a concrete
linear relation---and renders the corresponding diagram as it goes---would
be a genuine contribution: an executable, inspectable bridge between the
textual and the diagrammatic, with a live runtime attached.

Concretely, a generator set might read:

\begin{lstlisting}[style=kidlisp]
(seq                  ; sequential  o
  (par in-A in-B)     ; parallel    (x)
  add                 ; *  two in, one out
  copy                ; o  one in, two out
  (par out-1 out-2))
\end{lstlisting}

\noindent where evaluation produces both the matrix/relation it denotes
\emph{and} the drawn diagram---two views of one object, exactly GLA's
thesis made interactive.

\subsection{A signal-flow playground (the demonstrable one)}

Signal flow graphs are GLA's flagship application and are
\emph{intrinsically animated}: values stream down wires, scalars amplify,
feedback accumulates. AC is built for exactly this---real-time,
frame-driven, interactive. A piece where you wire generators on a canvas
and \textbf{watch the linear system run}---adjust a scalar and see the
trajectory change---is something the ACT community largely does not have.
It is the most compelling demo of all three because it shows the diagram
\emph{computing}, not just sitting.

\section{Why this is worth a paper, a tool, or a thesis}

The honest framing of the opportunity:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{For pedagogy:} GLA wants to be approachable---that is why
    the blog exists. Interactive, linkable diagrams lower the entry cost
    further than any PDF can.
  \item \textbf{For research:} \S3.2 is a real open-flavored problem at
    the intersection of programming-language design and category
    theory---a term language and live evaluator for props/linear
    relations.
  \item \textbf{For the field's culture:} ACT prizes \emph{working}
    compositional tools. A browser-native, shareable GLA playground is a
    contribution in a form the community values and currently lacks.
\end{itemize}

A fair caveat, stated plainly: a PhD in these groups is \textbf{formal
category theory}---props, monoidal categories, completeness proofs,
papers. The tool-building is an excellent entry credential and complement,
not a substitute for the abstract mathematics the degree demands. The
right posture is to lead with the artifact and learn the formalism, not
the reverse.

\section{The people and the path}

The GLA authors are the centre of gravity of Applied Category Theory:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Pawe\l{} Soboci\'nski}---Professor, \emph{TalTech}
    (Tallinn, Estonia); leads the Laboratory for Compositional Systems and
    Methods; supervises PhD students and coordinates a doctoral programme.
    Author of the GLA blog and an outreach-minded researcher. \textbf{The
    most natural first contact.}
  \item \textbf{Fabio Zanasi}---Professor, \emph{UCL} (London);
    co-author of the foundational GLA papers.
  \item \textbf{Filippo Bonchi}---\emph{University of Pisa}; the third
    co-author.
\end{itemize}

Two timely venues, both chaired by Soboci\'nski in Tallinn:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{ACT 2026} (Applied Category Theory), 6--10 July 2026;
  \item \textbf{DIAGRAMS 2026}, 24--28 August 2026---arguably the better
    fit for an interactive-diagrams contribution.
\end{itemize}

Estonian doctoral positions are \textbf{salaried}, sidestepping the
tuition-debt model of the US/UK.

\subsection*{Proposed first move}

Not ``may I do a PhD,'' but: \textbf{ship the smallest working demo and
point at it.} A KidLisp piece that renders and animates a GLA string
diagram (now in hand) plus a short note to Soboci\'nski---``here is what I
build; I'd like to build it for category theory; DIAGRAMS 2026?''---tests
the collaboration, funding, and thesis paths simultaneously, on the
strength of a running artifact rather than a cold ask. Practitioners in
this field notice tools.

\section{Kindred voices: Richard Southwell and the diagrammatic turn}

The case for an \emph{interactive} diagrammatic mathematics is not mine
alone. In his 2026 talk \emph{Speculations about the future of
mathematics}, the category-theory educator \textbf{Richard
Southwell}---author of \emph{Categories and Toposes: Visualized and
Explained}---argues that mathematics is shifting ``from this sort of
algebraic, encoded style of maths to something more like'' drawing
diagrams. His framing arrives, independently, at the exact metaphor
underpinning this paper and my whistlegraph practice:

\begin{quote}
\itshape ``I liken [algebra] to \textbf{sheet music}---as opposed to [the
diagram], which is the \textbf{actual music}. This is what our visual
cortex can process.'' --- Richard Southwell
\end{quote}

His worked example \emph{is} the companion piece \texttt{gla.lisp}: ``a
number flows in from the left; the black node \textbf{copies} it; the
white node \textbf{adds}---put a 3 in, get a 3 and a 3, add them, get 6.''
He closes with a plea that is, almost verbatim, the mission of Aesthetic
Computer: that people who understand graphical methods should ``spend more
time trying to make it easier for the rest of us.'' Southwell---an
independent educator actively seeking collaborators who make the subject
visual---is plausibly a lower-friction first contact than a cold email to
a department.

\textbf{A calibration, stated honestly.} Southwell's talk pairs GLA with a
second, far more speculative program: Norman Wildberger's \emph{box
arithmetic}, which takes multisets (drawn as nested boxes, with
``anti-boxes'' for negatives) as a finitist foundation that rejects
infinities and the real numbers. The two are \textbf{not} the same tier.
GLA is rigorous, peer-reviewed, and complete (the IHA theorem); box
arithmetic is heterodox, ultrafinitist, and---by Southwell's own
admission---``the wild west,'' not even proven consistent. For a serious
collaboration or thesis pitch, build on GLA. Box arithmetic is better
treated as provocation and art-fuel. Notably, its nested-box structure is
\emph{operadic}---rooted \textbf{trees}---which places it on the opposite
side of the same tree-vs-graph divide drawn in \S3.2: GLA is the graph
side, box arithmetic the tree side, and AC's score practice straddles
both.

\section{Status and next steps}

\begin{itemize}
  \item[$\checkmark$] \texttt{gla.lisp}---animated signal-flow string
    diagram, live in AC.
  \item[$\circ$] Generalise from hand-placed coordinates to a small

  \item[$\circ$] Prototype the \S3.2 term language: \texttt{seq} /

\end{itemize}
