\essaytitle{The Keymap Is the Score}
\begin{center}\includegraphics[width=0.46\textwidth]{../arxiv-keymaps/notepat-keymap}\end{center}\par\vspace{0.35em}
\begin{center}{\itshape\color{acgray}Keymaps as social software, and the edition we made for Cycle~2}\end{center}
\essaydate{June 2026}

A keymap is a small table. It says that the QWERTY key A plays one note, that S plays the next --- that the letters under your fingers are also musical pitches. It contains no code. It isn't a program you install or a thing that runs in the background. It's just a list of agreements about what each key means. And it's software in every way that counts: people version it, invent it, copy it and change it, carry it from one app to the next, and complain when an app leaves it out.

I first made one for \np{} --- a small piece I wrote in JavaScript on \ac{}. Since then the same keymap has shown up again and again: in the \ac{} operating system, in the \np{} instrument for upcycled computers, and in the Mac Menu Band app I built with Esteban Uribe. The notes stay the same; only the thing wrapped around them changes. That portability is the whole point --- and the reason I think a keymap is worth a paper.

This is the short version of why.

\section{A thing without a name}

A keymap is the layer everyone uses and nobody looks at. It's the QWERTY-to-piano mapping hidden inside every music app. It's the Vim commands that move from one text editor to the next. It's the Rubik's cube notation that lets a stranger in another country hand you a twenty-move solution on a napkin. None of these are programs, and none of them are data. They're agreements that act like software --- and the words we normally reach for can't quite see them. Asking whether a keymap is ``open source'' or ``proprietary'' makes no sense, because there's no source code underneath. It isn't a file format either: there's usually no official spec written down anywhere.

A thing without a name is hard to see, hard to argue about, and impossible to point at. So the first thing the paper does is give this category a name. I call it \textit{social software} --- and I mean three things at once. I mean Clay Shirky's 2003 idea: software made for being social. I mean this program, the Social Software program at UCLA Design Media Arts, where I'm writing this. And I mean a third meaning the first two leave room for: \textit{software that exists only because people agree it does.} A keymap is real because enough people decided to treat it as real. Stop agreeing, and it's gone --- not shut down, not retired, just no longer true.

\section{The case in the middle}

The clearest example is also the worst-designed one. Open almost any music app and you can play your computer keyboard like a piano: A is C, W is C-sharp, S is D, and on up a staircase of letters that have nothing to do with the notes they make. Nobody owns it. No company invented it and no patent covers it; every program includes it because every other program includes it. It's a near-perfect example of social software. And it's bad. It uses two hands' worth of keys for a single octave, and you can't figure it out without a tutorial. Nobody chose it --- it just piled up over time.

So I gave it a name: AWSED, after the keys A-W-S-E-D that spell its first five notes (it rhymes with gaming's WASD on purpose). The name is the whole trick: once it has one, you can say ``AWSED is bad'' in three words, and three words is short enough to travel. \np{} is my answer to it --- a keymap I designed on purpose instead of inheriting by accident. The letter keys play the notes they're named after: C plays C, D plays D, E plays E, and the sharps sit on the keys in between. There's one rule I call the contract: the basic note-to-key mapping is exactly the same everywhere \np{} runs. On top of that, each device is free to add its own extras. Break the shared part and you've broken \np{}; add your own flourishes above it and that's just personal style. A keymap you need a manual to use has stopped being social software.

Play it for yourself at \href{https://notepat.com}{notepat.com} --- click a key, or just type. The letter keys play the notes they name.

\section{A room full of scores}

Cycle~2 of Social Software is called \textit{Scores for Social Software}, led by Casey Reas, and it asked each of us to make a score --- a set of instructions for how something happens over time, written as text, diagrams, or directions. The tradition behind it runs through the Fluxus event score, Cage and Knowles' \textit{Notations}, Hans Ulrich Obrist's \textit{do it}, and the work Natilee Harren shared with us through The Scores Project. I didn't have to leave my paper to answer the prompt, because a keymap is a score. It's an instruction for a body --- this finger, this key, this note --- that does nothing until someone performs it, and only becomes social once it's written down in a form you can hand to the next person.

That last part is what the paper cares about most. A keymap actually has two halves: the rule (A plays C), and the way you write that rule down so it can travel. Vim has both --- \texttt{:wq} is something you can say out loud, type, and teach. The music-app staircase has the rule but no good way to write it down, which is exactly why it never got better. If you can't pass the written form along, there's nothing for people to share, argue over, and improve. So shipping only the key-to-note table is shipping half the thing. The paper ends with one small, specific ask: treat the keymap as a real, first-class object --- something you can read, export, and copy-and-change --- and share its written form right alongside it.

\section{The edition}

This page goes with the first numbered print edition of the paper, made in June 2026 as my piece of Cycle~2. The edition is itself a kind of social contract: every person in the cohort made sixty-four copies of their own score, and on June~13, 2026, at Fuser --- 1811 Blake Ave, in Frogtown --- we brought them all into one room and gathered them into a single publication. One full set per copy, sixty-four times over. A paper about agreements that only exist because people act on them, bound by hand into an object that only exists because a room full of people showed up and agreed to make it.

Everyone arrived with a score: Jordan Silver's \textit{Sonic Architecture}, Em Lugo's \textit{Ambling Los Angeles}, Æther Cavendish's \textit{Vigil Score}, Thomas Noya's \textit{Biophonía}, Mavyn Vu's \textit{the radio is an altar : portal}, Alex Espinosa's \textit{Music for World Computers}, Darlyn Phan's \textit{Meridians}, Chelly Jin's \textit{Software as a Choreography}, Banyi Huang's \textit{Conjuring Notness}, and mine --- the keymaps paper, under the banner of \ac{}. The cycle was convened by Casey Reas and Lauren Lee McCarthy, and produced with Wylie Kasai. Reading these scores side by side is the best argument I can make: each one is a social contract --- an agreement about what people do next --- and a keymap is the same kind of thing, a contract about what a key means. None of us shipped a program you run, and all of us shipped software.

The keymap I designed for \href{https://notepat.com}{notepat.com} is still right there on its front page. It still contains no code. It's still just a table of agreements --- which, it turns out, is one of the oldest and most durable kinds of software we have. It only needed a name.

And as a little sign-off gift for this write-up: a simpler version of that keymap lives at the foot of the online edition, written in p5.js, so you can play it right there.
