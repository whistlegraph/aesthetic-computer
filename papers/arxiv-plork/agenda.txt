PLORKing the Planet — Paper Agenda
===================================
Jeffrey Alan Scudder / Aesthetic Inc.
Started: 2026-03-17

THESIS
------
PLOrk (Princeton Laptop Orchestra, est. 2005) proved that laptop ensembles
are a legitimate performance and pedagogical form — but it did so at Princeton
prices: custom hemispherical speakers, MacBooks, 8-channel amps, racks, and
institutional infrastructure. Twenty years later, the laptop orchestra model
remains trapped inside universities. L2Ork (Virginia Tech) tried to cut costs
with Linux netbooks ($750/seat vs PLOrk's ~$1500+/seat) but still required
custom speaker builds and institutional support.

AC Native OS changes the equation entirely. A single 89 MB file turns any
surplus x86_64 laptop ($30–80) into a dedicated musical instrument with
built-in synthesis, network connectivity, personalization, and OTA updates.
No hemispherical speakers needed — the laptop IS the instrument. No ChucK,
no Max/MSP, no Pure Data install — the runtime is the OS. No IT department —
the devices update themselves over WiFi.

With 240 million PCs rendered "obsolete" by Windows 10 end-of-life (Oct 2025),
and 62 million tonnes of e-waste generated globally per year, the raw material
for a planetary laptop orchestra already exists. It's sitting in warehouses,
school closets, and e-waste bins. The question is not whether we can afford
to PLOrk the planet — it's whether we can afford not to.

CRITICAL STANCE
---------------
- PLOrk is beautiful but elitist: Princeton endowment, custom hardware,
  enrollment-gated access
- L2Ork improved cost but remained institutional
- SLOrk, CLOrk, BLOrk etc. are all university-bound
- The laptop orchestra movement peaked at ~30 ensembles worldwide, all academic
- Sonic Pi democratized music coding but still needs a host OS + speakers
- OLPC tried hardware-first global access and failed ($100 became $188+)
- Chromebook "churn" costs schools $1.8 billion in premature disposal
- 240 million Windows 10 PCs about to become e-waste = largest surplus event
  in computing history

PAPER STRUCTURE
---------------

1. INTRODUCTION — The PLOrk Dream and Its Limits
   - PLOrk (2005): Dan Trueman, Perry Cook, Ge Wang
   - 15 stations, hemispherical speakers, ChucK/Max/MSP
   - Spawned 30+ university laptop orchestras worldwide
   - But: all academic, all institutional, all expensive
   - The dream of a laptop orchestra was always a dream of access
   - Twenty years later: access is still gated

2. THE LAPTOP ORCHESTRA LANDSCAPE
   - PLOrk: ~$1500+/seat (MacBook + hemi speaker + amp rack + controllers)
   - L2Ork: ~$750/seat (Linux netbooks + cheaper hemis + Pd-L2Ork)
   - SLOrk: Stanford, ChucK, IKEA salad bowl speakers
   - CLOrk, BLOrk, HELO, OLO, SCLOrk, etc.
   - Common requirements: institutional affiliation, equipment budget,
     dedicated space, technical staff, semester-long commitment
   - Sonic Pi: free software but still needs hardware + OS + speakers
   - Total worldwide laptop orchestra participants: maybe 500–1000/year?

3. THE SURPLUS TSUNAMI
   - Windows 10 EOL (Oct 14, 2025): 240 million PCs declared obsolete
   - UN Global E-waste Monitor 2024: 62 Mt/year, only 22% recycled
   - Chromebook churn: schools replacing millions of devices every 3–5 years
   - Oakland USD alone: 40,000 Chromebooks expiring
   - Corporate refresh cycles: ThinkPads, EliteBooks, Latitudes at $30–80
   - These machines have: 1080p screens, 4–8 cores, 8–16 GB RAM, WiFi,
     keyboards, speakers, 6–10 hr batteries
   - The hardware for a million laptop orchestras already exists

4. AC NATIVE OS AS INSTRUMENT
   - Architecture: single 89 MB UEFI binary, boots in 7.3 seconds
   - No userspace, no desktop, no package manager
   - 11,943-line C runtime as PID 1
   - QuickJS JavaScript engine for piece execution
   - Built-in: 32-voice synth, ALSA audio, WiFi, camera, TTS, WebSocket
   - Device-level personalization: config.json on EFI partition
   - OTA updates preserve identity across software evolution
   - Piece ecosystem: keyboard instruments, sequencers, visualizers, AI chat

5. FROM ENSEMBLE TO PLANET — The PLOrk Scaling Problem
   - PLOrk serves 15 people per semester at Princeton
   - Scaling PLOrk means: more speakers, more amps, more rack gear, more space
   - Scaling AC OS means: more USB drives
   - Cost comparison table:
     | Approach              | 30-seat | 100-seat | 1000-seat | 10000-seat |
     | PLOrk model           | $45,000 | $150,000 | impossible | impossible |
     | L2Ork model           | $22,500 | $75,000  | impossible | impossible |
     | AC OS + surplus       | $1,500  | $5,000   | $50,000    | $500,000   |
   - At AC OS prices, a city-wide laptop orchestra costs less than
     one PLOrk setup
   - At AC OS prices, a national laptop orchestra is cheaper than
     a school computer lab

6. PLORK THE PLANET — A Concrete Proposal
   - Community flashing events: bring your dead laptop, leave with an instrument
   - School programs: surplus laptops from corporate donors, flashed with AC OS
   - After-school ensembles: no equipment budget needed
   - Remote/rural access: OTA updates work on any WiFi, even tethered phones
   - International: the same USB image works on any x86_64 machine anywhere
   - Mesh networking (future): peer-to-peer ensemble without internet
   - The PLOrk model inverted: instead of bringing people to the orchestra,
     bring the orchestra to the people

7. WHAT THE LAPTOP ORCHESTRA TAUGHT US (AND WHAT IT MISSED)
   - PLOrk proved: laptop ensembles are musically legitimate
   - PLOrk proved: spatialized sound matters (hemispherical speakers)
   - PLOrk proved: new instruments need new compositions
   - PLOrk missed: accessibility, global reach, non-institutional contexts
   - L2Ork proved: Linux + cheaper hardware works
   - L2Ork missed: still university-bound, still needs speaker builds
   - Sonic Pi proved: code-as-music is pedagogically powerful
   - Sonic Pi missed: still needs a host OS, still a single-user tool
   - AC OS synthesizes all lessons: instrument-grade audio on bare metal,
     network-native ensemble, zero-cost software, near-zero hardware cost
