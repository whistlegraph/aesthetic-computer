A single brass institutional donor-wall plaque, shown straight-on and perfectly centered, floating. One solid rectangular cast-brass tablet with a softly bevelled edge and a warm patina, mounted with two small visible screws at the top corners. Its face is divided into a stack of engraved horizontal name-bands — eight or nine slim ruled rows, each holding the rhythm of an engraved donor name rendered as illegible scribble-gestalt, never readable words. A faint engraved hairline rule separates a slightly taller top band (the dedication line) from the donor rows below. The brass catches a low raking light: high specular contrast and crisp incised shadow in the engraving at the center, the metal and its rows dissolving softly to nothing at the periphery. No picket sign, no fist, no flag, no protest iconography. No human figures. No hands. Nothing held in front of it. The plaque IS the entire image — the institution's own self-display turned into the material of the critique.
