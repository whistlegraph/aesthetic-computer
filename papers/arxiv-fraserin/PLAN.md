# Research Plan · arxiv-fraserin

**Working title**: *A Fraserin' Art + Tech*

**Long-form subtitle (working)**: *Andrea-Fraser as a verb, sung at art-and-tech: what the move would mean, and to what extent it's already been done.*

**Status**: Plan only — no draft yet. Folder created 2026-05-02. Slug `arxiv-fraserin`.
**Author**: @jeffrey
**Mode**: Methodology essay + literature survey, written in a labor-folk register. There IS a question here, unlike the dossier papers. The question has two halves: (a) what would it mean to apply Andrea Fraser's institutional-critique grammar to the art-and-tech cluster, and (b) to what extent has this already been done — by whom, in what form, and where the gaps are.

---

## Voice (the folk-tune register)

The title is read in the cadence of a Woody-Guthrie / Pete-Seeger / Almanac Singers labor song: "A-Workin' on the Railroad," "A-Pickin' and a-Grinnin'," "I've Been A-Travelin'." That cadence is not decoration. It carries the paper's argument, because:

- Fraser's late work *is* class politics — *Museums, Money, and Politics* (2018) is a labor / donor-class ledger; *L'1%, c'est moi* (2011) is a wage-and-wealth pamphlet.
- The art-and-tech cluster's specifics (residency labor, fellowship sub-market wages, volunteer admins, unpaid mailing-list moderators, alumni-as-network) are *worker* questions, not just curatorial ones.
- W.A.G.E., Ukeles' *Manifesto for Maintenance Art*, the IWW Cultural Workers tradition, and the New Museum / MoMA / Whitney staff unionizations of 2019–2024 all sit on this axis.
- The folk-song form is itself an institutional-critique tradition: the Almanac Singers sang the union into existence; the song *is* the picket line.

So the prose follows. Plain American English. Short lines. A recurring refrain. The six moves of Fraser's grammar can structure the verses; the refrain can be the title or a variant of it. Section headings can be folk-song style ("Verse 1 — The Word", "Refrain", "Chorus"). The paper can literally open with a short lyric and then proceed in prose — that is Fraser's (4) "embody where possible" move, satisfied by form.

This is a real choice and may not survive the first draft. But the rename signals it as the working register.

---

## What this is

Not another dossier. The dossier series (Eyebeam, Rhizome, SFPC, Recurse, Pioneer Works, Mellon, Internet Archive) is the *evidence layer*. This paper is the *lens layer* — it names the move the dossiers are already making, traces it to Fraser's grammar, and asks what is specifically owed by — and possible for — the **art-and-tech cluster** under that grammar.

(Throughout: "art-and-tech" is the working umbrella term for the cluster also called *media arts*, *new media art*, *digital art*, *creative coding*, *software art*, *electronic art*. The labels are partially overlapping and historically contested. The paper takes "art-and-tech" as the broadest container and uses the narrower terms only where the source it's drawing from used them.)

Two halves of the paper:

1. **What it would mean** — methodological reflection. What does "Andrea Frasering" entail? Which of Fraser's moves transfer to art-and-tech institutions and which don't? What does this cluster (Rhizome, Eyebeam, SFPC, NEW INC, Recurse, ZKM, Ars Electronica, V2_, Furtherfield, Babycastles, Pioneer Works, etc.) make newly possible or newly hard?

2. **Has it been done** — prior-art survey. Where has institutional critique of art-and-tech institutions already happened, by whom, in what register (book, essay, exhibition, art piece, list, leak, lawsuit), and to what depth? Where are the gaps the AC dossier series is — knowingly or not — filling?

## What "Andrea Frasering" means as a verb

A working definition, to be sharpened in the paper. Drawing on Fraser's body of work and her 2005 Artforum essay *From the Critique of Institutions to an Institution of Critique*:

1. **Treat the institution as the subject**, not as the neutral container for artworks. Genealogy, governance, money, donors, board, programs, people, footprint — these are the material.
2. **Refuse the outside**. The critique is performed from within, by a participant. There is no neutral exterior position; the critic is also the critiqued.
3. **Surface money, class, donor base, and governance as art-historical material**, not as metadata or footnote. The 990, the masthead, the donor wall, the board affiliations are the work.
4. **Embody where possible**. Fraser's strongest pieces are not external analyses but performances *as* the institution's roles — docent, gallerist, member, commissioned artist. The form mimics the function.
5. **Accept reproduction**. The critique cannot escape the institution it names; it gets absorbed and resold. Proceed anyway, with that fact visible.
6. **Report the ledger**. *Museums, Money, and Politics* (2018, with Westreich) just *prints* the political donations of every US museum board member. The paper *is* the table.

The dossier series in `papers/` already does (1), (3), and partly (6). It does not yet do (2), (4), (5) deliberately. This paper would name those gaps and decide which to close.

## Why art-and-tech institutions specifically

The art-and-tech cluster has features Fraser's home turf (MoMA, the Wadsworth, the Whitney) doesn't:

- **Tech-capital entanglement** — boards and donor lists run through Google, Microsoft, Adobe, Bloomberg, Knight, Mellon, Sloan, the venture-capital corridor, defense contractors. The donor wall is also a tech industry ledger.
- **Fellowship and residency labor** — much of the program output is volunteer or sub-market labor (residencies, fellowships, "honoraria"). The accession isn't a $50M painting; it's a person and 3–6 months of their time.
- **Software as the work** — what is being archived is unstable: ArtBase needs Webrecorder, Eyebeam projects bit-rot, SFPC student work is on Glitch. Conservation is a budget line and a politics.
- **Education-as-program** — SFPC, Recurse, NEW INC blur "school" and "residency" and "incubator". The pedagogy is a product, the alumni are the network, and the network is the funding case.
- **The artist is often also the engineer, the curator, the donor target, and the platform user**. The participant categories collapse — exactly the condition Fraser's late work names ("we are the institution").

These features mean the Fraser grammar transfers, but with different stress points. The paper enumerates them.

## §4 · Prior art — has it been done? Survey skeleton

The honest answer is: **partially, scattered, never named as a coherent project.** Below are the strands to research, with first-pass guesses about depth and where to look. Everything here needs verification during the writing pass.

### Strand A — Andrea Fraser herself, on art-and-tech

To my knowledge Fraser has not made a specifically-art-and-tech-institution piece. *Museums, Money, and Politics* (2018) covers US art museums broadly; some have media-arts programs (Whitney, MoMA, SFMOMA, Walker) but the methodology is not aimed at the art-and-tech cluster as such. Worth checking: any Whitney Biennial / Performa / Tate Modern lectures or essays where she discussed digital, software, or net practice. **Likely gap. Verify.**

### Strand B — Critics inside the cluster who already do institutional critique

Candidates to read closely:

- **Domenico Quaranta** — *Beyond New Media Art* (2013) and *In Your Computer* (2011) (both already in the AC readings library, Link Editions CC). The 2013 book is essentially a critique of how the new-media-art ghetto perpetuates itself institutionally. Closest existing analogue.
- **Geert Lovink** — long arc of Institute of Network Cultures essays and books. Sustained critique of the digital-art world's organizational forms (festivals, biennales, mailing lists). Probably the deepest single body of work on this question. Acquire *Networks Without a Cause* (2011), *Social Media Abyss* (2016), *Sad by Design* (2019).
- **Josephine Bosma** — *Nettitudes* (2011). Critical position on net art including its institutions.
- **Tatiana Bazzichelli** — *Networked Disruption* (2013). Hacker-art and capital.
- **Cornell & Halter (eds.)** — *Mass Effect* (MIT Press, 2015). On the SoSoft acquisition list. Rhizome's own theoretical statement; partly self-critical.
- **Karen Archey & Robin Peckham** — *Art Post-Internet* (UCCA, 2014). Catalog with critical apparatus.
- **Christiane Paul** — *Digital Art* (Thames & Hudson). Multiple editions; institutional history embedded.
- **Inke Arns** — HMKV Dortmund curatorial essays. German-Austrian-Dutch institutional context.
- **Tilman Baumgärtel** — *net.art 2.0* (2001). Documents early list-and-festival institutional formation.

Verdict to test: this strand has done (1) and partially (3) of the Fraser grammar, but rarely (2), (4), (5), (6). It's mostly *external* critique by participants who don't name themselves as inside.

### Strand C — Artists doing institutional-critique-style works *about* art-and-tech institutions

- **Constant Dullaart** — *High Retention, Slow Delivery* (2014) and follow-ons. Institutional critique of platform metrics, by buying and distributing 2.5M Instagram followers among the art world.
- **Paolo Cirio** — *Loophole for All*, *Daily Paywall*, *Sociality*. Finance and platform critique; often crosses into media-arts venues.
- **!Mediengruppe Bitnik** — *Random Darknet Shopper*, *Delivery for Mr. Assange*. Institutional and platform critique with art-world placement.
- **Olia Lialina** — *A Vernacular Web*, *Turing Complete User*. Critique of platform vernacular but also of the institutional capture of net art history.
- **Andrew Norman Wilson** — *Workers Leaving the Googleplex*. Labor/institutional critique of tech, shown in art venues.
- **Hito Steyerl** — *Duty Free Art* (2017), the "freeport" essay especially. Institutional-financial critique. Crosses into media-arts venues but aims at art-finance broadly.
- **Trevor Paglen** — surveillance and infrastructure as institutional subject.
- **Cory Arcangel** — *Working On My Novel* and the Whitney show as a self-aware institutional move. More performative-institutional than critical.
- **Goldin+Senneby** — finance/institution as material.

Verdict to test: this strand does (4) embodied moves and (6) reporting, but mostly aimed at *tech platforms* or *art-finance broadly*, rarely at art-and-tech *institutions* specifically.

### Strand D — Activist / archival / list-based work

- **Decolonize This Place** and **Strike MoMA** — direct-action critique of board affiliations (Warren Kanders, Larry Fink). Methodology partially overlaps with Fraser's *Museums, Money, and Politics*.
- **Forensic Architecture** — *Triple-Chaser* (2019) on Kanders. Institutional critique by evidence.
- **W.A.G.E.** (Working Artists and the Greater Economy) — fee-for-labor certification, institutional reform. Methodology = data + standards.
- **MTL Collective** / **Nitasha Dhillon & Amin Husain** — earlier work on board affiliations.
- **Rhizome editorial** itself — periodic pieces on the conditions of net art's institutional life. Could be coded as auto-critique.
- **Artists Space, MoMA PS1, e-flux journal** — recurring institutional self-reflection in print. *e-flux journal* has run pieces by Steyerl, Aranda, Vidokle that touch this.

Verdict to test: this strand does (3) and (6) at high resolution (the Kanders dossier was essentially a Fraser-style ledger) but is rarely aimed at *art-and-tech* institutions. Closest analogue methodologically.

### Strand E — Inside-academic institutional-critique writing

- **Alexander Alberro & Blake Stimson (eds.)** — *Institutional Critique: An Anthology of Artists' Writings* (MIT Press, 2009). The canonical anthology. Probably has zero art-and-tech institutions in scope.
- **Andrea Fraser**, "From the Critique of Institutions to an Institution of Critique" (Artforum, Sept 2005). The canonical essay.
- **Pierre Bourdieu**, *The Rules of Art* (1996) and *Distinction* (1984). The sociological substrate Fraser draws on.
- **Howard Becker**, *Art Worlds* (1982). Networked-institution sociology.
- **Hans Haacke** — *Shapolsky et al. Manhattan Real Estate Holdings* (1971). The pre-Fraser ledger move.

Verdict to test: this strand provides the grammar but does not apply it to art-and-tech institutions. The application is exactly the gap.

### Strand G — Labor / cultural-worker tradition (the folk-tune lineage)

This strand is not usually grouped with institutional critique but the paper proposes it should be. The labor-folk register makes this lineage visible:

- **Mierle Laderman Ukeles** — *Manifesto for Maintenance Art* (1969). The maintenance-as-art frame is the labor side of the same coin Fraser flips. Already cited in `arxiv-ac` and `arxiv-pieces`.
- **W.A.G.E.** — fee-for-labor certification. Standards-as-critique.
- **Working Artists Coalition / IWW Cultural Workers** — historical labor organizing in cultural sectors.
- **Almanac Singers / Pete Seeger / Woody Guthrie** — the song-as-picket-line tradition the title invokes.
- **MoMA Local 2110, New Museum Union, Whitney Workers United** — staff unionizations 2019–2024 at exactly the institutions Fraser critiqued.
- **Ben Davis** — *9.5 Theses on Art and Class* (2013). Class analysis of contemporary art labor.
- **Helen Molesworth** — curator-as-worker writings.
- **Andrew Ross** — *Nice Work If You Can Get It* (2009), *No-Collar* (2003). Cultural-labor scholarship.
- **Silvio Lorusso** — *Entreprecariat* (2019). Labor under platform capitalism, with art-and-tech in scope.

Verdict to test: this strand is the labor-side complement to Fraser's class-side. The folk-tune title is what makes the strand visible inside the project.

## What this paper is NOT

- Not a Fraser biography. Cite her, don't profile her.
- Not a dossier. The institutions aren't the subject; the *method of looking at them* is.
- Not a hit list. Naming gaps is not naming targets.
- Not a manifesto. The mode is reflective: "what would it mean", not "we must".
- Not exhaustive. The prior-art survey will miss things; flag gaps inline and move on.
