A single hand-thrown Georgian salt-glazed earthenware jug, the kind an 18th-century Glasgow merchant potter would have turned at his wheel: a bellied stoneware vessel with a pulled handle, a slightly off-round lip, and the faint spiral throwing-rings left by fingers in wet clay. The mottled grey-brown glaze has the orange-peel texture of salt firing, warming to ochre near the base. Around the belly of the jug, incised cleanly into the clay before firing, runs a single horizontal string-line marked with small division ticks at unequal intervals — like a monochord's measured string wrapped around the pot, the only ornament on its surface. The jug sits alone, three-quarter view, its rounded silhouette unmistakable, edges dissolving into bare cream paper.
